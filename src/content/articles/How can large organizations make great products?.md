Boeing, in 1968, with 148,672 employees, produced the 747, the greatest airliner of all time. 

---

It's conventional wisdom that big organizations make subpar products. My boss literally [wrote the book](https://www.amazon.com/Deadly-Memos-Conventional-Companies-Pamphlet-ebook/dp/B09G5XX8MW) on this problem! But size is only correlated with failure. The failure of scale can be escaped. There are *really damn good* products that are designed and made by big bureaucracies. **None** are explained by the typical stories: a founder-dictator, a skunkworks group, a heroic individual, an unusual license to create.

- Nintendo, a Japanese company founded in 1889, with 7,000 employees, produced *Breath of the Wild*, widely known as one of the greatest video games of all time. They sold more than 35 million copies, making more than $1.5B. From one game.
- Pixar, between 1995-2015, won eighteen academy awards, ten golden globes, and eleven grammies. They won Best Animated Feature 11 times.
- Anthropic has a headcount of at least 2,500. Anthropic is likely the [fastest growing company of all time](https://www.axios.com/2026/04/13/anthropic-revenue-growth-ai), beating wartime manufacturing, railroads, and oil. They released Claude Code in May 2025, and it made $2.5B of revenue within a year.
- Lego is a $9B company that makes the most loved products in the world.

So what's the trick? How does Lego make delightful, beloved products, while Atlassian has a [community built around shared hatred](https://ifuckinghatejira.com) of their flagship product? How does Boeing produce the "queen of the skies," and then go on to produce the disastrous 737 MAX?

**The job of a great product organization is to keep tasteful people in contact with reality and give them final say.**

Fujibayashi played *Breath of the Wild* obsessively during development and cut what he didn't like. Boeing's chief engineers flew their own test planes. Anthropic's Claude Code team uses Claude Code to build Claude Code.

---
### How to make a great product

- Accept that excellence is illegible. Allow important decisions to be defended with "because it's better" rather than data.
- Force confrontation with the real product. Pixar had dailies. Toyota had gemba walks. Lego designers make their own sets with physical lego bricks. If you can't use your product, go watch the people who do.
- Work against real deadlines, feedback, and constraints. Artificial constraints, imagined user feedback from executives, and arbitrary deadlines don't work.
  
---
In the failure mode of Atlassian or Workday, you implement all user requests. Users have *no idea* what makes a good product-- go read any video game forum for proof-- but nobody says "no" to a request. Nobody is differentiating between what should and should not be built. You don't have a tastemaker who is calling the shots, making an opinionated product, and alienating some users. You're gathering feedback! You're measuring dau and wau and pirate metrics! But it is unfortunately causing you more harm than good. You create an infinitely flexible product that does everything and nothing and is incredibly frustrating for all, and loved by none.

In the other, more common, failure mode, the engineers don't get real feedback at *all*. Someone drafts a spec, and you spend months working on the spec in isolation. It is released to little fanfare. This is what "agile" is meant to solve, but nobody actually gets user feedback. Instead, you work on a pre-specced project that you pretend isn't pre-specced two weeks at a time, and you call the waterfall an "epic," the SVP is happy with the burndown rate, and you release a product 4 months later to fanfare from your manager that no one uses.

Scale makes proxy metrics tempting because real feedback becomes expensive. When you have 10,000 users, it becomes *expensive* to get real, noisy feedback, and you instead melt all of the invaluable user experience into a meaningless Net Promoter Score. The larger your organization grows, the more clamor you will hear for KPIs and other malignant proxies for excellence. Worse, you start to make key decisions using these proxies for excellence. **There is no proxy metric for excellence.** Then, you get hit by one of the following two failure modes.

To prevent both, you need your engineers and designers to get real feedback, constantly, and have the authority to reject it.

Almost all organizations, at scale, are *deeply* uncomfortable with signals that can't be quantified. Rather than accepting that some signals aren't legible, they pretend illegible signals don't exist. This is why I *hate* using dashboards to manage products. Dashboards pretend product decisions are a function of legible signals. But you cannot reduce great product decisions to a KPI. You can't measure most of the things that accountants pretend to, because attribution is impossible. The worst case outcome is when managers institute change management to reduce variance in product quality, and completely amputate the organizations ability to make amazing things.
